Prior to the experiment explain all the relevant procedures and give sufficient information for your participant to give his/her informed consent. Please adhere to the departmental guidelines on ethics and informed consent. The relevant documentation is in the ‘Ethics’ folder on d-mail. Signed consent forms should be stored in the filing cabinet in the lab. This cabinet should be locked, with the key stored in a secure place. For experiments that are run in multiple sessions, keep a spreadsheet that links the participant’s identity to their “code name”. Use the code name to store their data (e.g. participant 1, block 2, condition 3: P1_2_3). Once you have finished that particular experiment, delete the spreadsheet (completely, also from the waste basket!).

 

Regardless of which libraries are used to run your experiment, a call to the experimental program will run a single block of trials. Make sure that the specified file name for the .edf file created by the operator PC (normally specified when calling the program in Matlab) is 8 characters or less. On the operator PC you need to check a number of settings.

 

Camera Setup

  • Tracking mode: Pupil - CR
  • Sample Rate: 2K (1K if you need better resolution)
  • Pupil tracking: Centroid
  • Camera Position: select the correct eye or click 'Camera Position Detect'
  • You may have to click on the large image at the top of the screen to select the correct "eye region" within which the system searches for the pupil

 

Set Options

  • Calibration Type: typically 9-point grid (2nd from right)
  • Pacing interval: OFF (best done manually)
  • The four buttons below should be 'on': Randomize Order, Force Manual Accept, Repeat First Point, Camera Position Detect
  • Tracking: Search Limits and Move Limits on
  • Events and Data Processing
    • Eye Event Data: Gaze
    • Saccade sensitivity depends on the application
    • File Sample Filter: EXTRA
    • Link/Analog Filter: STD
  • File Data Contents: both Samples and Events on
  • File Sample Contents: only Gaze Position on
  • Record View: pretty irrelevant, but Gaze Cursor is on by default
  • Plot: Gaze
  • All other buttons and those that were not mentioned can be turned off

 

Once you have chosen the correct settings for your experiment, you are ready to go. First make sure your participant is as comfortable as possible by adjusting the chair height, as well as the z and y position of the chinrest. The centre of the monitor should be aligned with the line of sight when looking straight ahead. The monitor is positioned at 57 cm away from the eyes (so that 1 cm corresponds to 1 degree of visual angle).

 

On the operator monitor, the top image of the participant's face should have the eyes vertically centred. Next, position the mirror so as to obtain a good image from the participant’s dominant eye. It's undoubtedly better to ask the participant to move their chair and chinrest up or down so that the mirror can be placed in the middle of its range than to just tilt the mirror to its extremes, especially if the participant wears glasses.** Use the handle at the top of the tower to focus the image.

 

Provided you have both a pupil and CR signal that is stable even as the observer is looking at the four corners of the screen, you are ready to proceed with the calibration and validation routine. After validating, check the average error (i.e. difference between the two successive calibrations) and make sure it is below 0.5º. This is an arbitrary, but useful rule of the thumb. If the error is larger, re-run the calibration and validation. For each participant, make a note of their gender and age, as well as their calibration accuracies for each block.

 

If you cannot obtain a good calibration from an observer you have the choice of either abandoning the experiment (a good idea when high accuracy is required and/or the experiment is run in multiple sessions) or cut your losses and test the participant. If you proceed with testing despite a poor calibration you may still want to include the data if the accuracy requirements are not very strict. Otherwise, you will just have to waste your own time by testing and then discarding the data. Under some circumstances it may be preferable to “test and discard” if abandoning the experiment is likely to upset your participant. Although I cannot think of many circumstances in which this would be the case, please take care to display some sensitivity towards your participants’ needs.

 

Very occasionally, the lack of a good calibration is due a fault in the machine. You can tell if this is the case if the calibration screen

  1. Constantly displays the yellow 'pupil size' error on the right of the screen when the pupil looks fine on the camera.
  2. Almost constantly displays the 'MOVING' warning in the bottom right of the screen when the pupil appears stationary.

 

In these and only these cases, try deleting the lastrun.ini and *.cal files from the elcl\exe directory on the host computer. To do this, restart the right-hand PC in Windows (by clicking the Windows icon instead of the eyelink one). Click on 'My Computer' and open F:\\ELCL\EXE and delete lastrun.ini and any files ending in .cal. Restart the computer again, letting Eyelink load this time and try the calibration again.

 

Regardless of the method of reward (experimental hours or monetary), it is only fair to pay them for their time, even when the experiment had to be abandoned. However, in this case you obviously only reward the participant for the time actually spent trying to get the experiment going (e.g. half an hour). Never be too stingy when it comes to rewarding your participants: Remember that they are doing you a favour. So round upwards (to the nearest half hour or £5) when it comes to calculating their reward.

 

A block of trials typically consists of ~100 trials and will take 5 – 8 minutes to complete. I do not recommend using longer blocks than that. Allow participants breaks in between blocks. It is important that your participant keeps his/her head as stable as possible within the tower mount during a block. Do not allow breaks during a block (unless the participant simply closes his/her eyes for a brief period). Especially with young, undergraduate students it is worth stating this upfront and being quite strict about it.

 

Once you've the finished the experiment, it's time for the fun part: analysing the data.

 

**If you are testing someone who wears glasses, make sure the search limits are turned on in 'Set Options'. There should then be a large red circle around the eye in the Camera Setup screen. Alter the size of the limits using Alt + directional buttons to make them small enough to miss any dark parts of the glasses (the frames etc) which might confuse the tracker, but large enough to cover the whole eye. Make sure the mirror is positioned so that any yellow patches are as far away from the pupil as possible and focus the image carefully. Ask the participant to look to the four corners of the screen to check that the pupil is still tracked. If it isn't, then alter a few things and try again. This is really difficult, so if it doesn't work after a few tries don't worry, just apologise and pay them for their time. It's generally easier to get the eye tracker to work with short-sighted people.
